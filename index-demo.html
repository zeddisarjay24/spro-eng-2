ONCE UPON A TIME – long, long ago – there was a queen who in winter was sitting by the open window embroidering. 'Ouch!' she cried suddenly, for

she had pricked her finger. A drop of blood fell on the snow under the window. 'I wish, I wish,' said the queen, 'that I had a daughter with a skin as white as this snow, with hair as black as the ebony of this window-frame, and with lips as red as this drop of blood.' And what do you think? After a while a daughter was

born, and the girl did indeed have hair as black as ebony, a skin as white as snow, and lips as red as blood. So the king and queen were quite happy, you understand, and they called their daughter Snow White.

 

 

But one day the queen became ill, even so badly that she died. The king, who did not like it, so alone, after a while married again.

His new wife was very beautiful, but what the king did not know was that she thought of nothing else all day long but being beautiful. In her room she even had a magic mirror for that purpose.

 

Every morning she would stand in front of this mirror and ask:

 

Mirror, mirror on the wall, who is the fairest of them all?

 

And because it was a magic mirror, he could talk, and he always answered:

 

You are the most beautiful in the land .

 

 

The queen then sat down contentedly on a chair in front of her dressing table to  make herself even more beautiful.

But one day things went differently. The queen stood in front of the mirror again and asked:

 

 

Mirror, mirror on the wall, who is the fairest of them all?

And do you know what she heard now?

 

Oh, Queen, you are the most beautiful in this room,

but Snow White is a thousand times more beautiful than you.

 

The queen could not believe her ears. She pushed aside the curtain and saw Snow White playing, and knew at once that the mirror was right. 'No one must be more beautiful than I,' the queen growled. And then she thought of something very evil.

 

She called the palace hunter to her and gave him a large knife. 'Tomorrow you will take Snow White into the forest,' she said, 'and there you will stab her to death with this knife.' Then she gave him a small box.

"And to make sure she's dead, I want this box back with Snow White's heart in it."

 

 

The next day the huntsman went with Snow White into the forest. But when he took out his knife, he felt sorry for her. 'Go away, Snow White,' he said, 'as far away as possible, because the queen wants me to kill you.' Frightened, Snow White ran as fast as she could, deep into the forest. And do you know what the huntsman did? He killed a wild boar and put its heart in the queen's box.

 

 

Meanwhile, Snow White kept running. She ran over seven mountains until, exhausted, she came to a tiny house. The front door was not locked.

Snow White had to duck when she went inside.

There must be seven children living here, she thought. Look, there are seven little chairs, seven little plates, seven little glasses and seven little spoons. She

walked up a small narrow staircase and at the top she saw seven little beds in a row. Suddenly she felt how tired she was. (Yes, what do you expect, when you have run so hard over seven mountains!) And before she knew it she was stretched out across three beds and fell into a deep sleep.

 

Because of that she didn't hear the residents coming home. They weren't seven

children but seven dwarfs, and they saw right away that someone had been in the house. They crept upstairs and discovered the sleeping Snow White.


"Oh, how sweet she is," said one dwarf. "And how beautiful," said another.

"Shhh, don't wake them!" said a third. And they quietly crept back down the stairs.

 

 

 

After a long time Snow White woke up and when she found the seven dwarfs downstairs, she told them what had happened to her. The dwarfs had heard of the evil queen. 'They say she can do magic,' they whispered and crept closer together.

 

The next morning the dwarves had to go to the silver mine to work as usual. At the door they said to Snow White: 'Be careful, don't let anyone in. You never know!' They waved to her and disappeared behind the mountains.

 

 

 

Meanwhile, in the palace, the hunter had given the box to the evil queen.

 

"Now I'm the most beautiful again," she said with satisfaction and quickly went to stand in front of the magic mirror.

 

Mirror, mirror on the wall, who is the fairest of them all?

 

The mirror replied:

 

Oh, Queen, you are the most beautiful in this room,

but Snow White is a thousand times more beautiful than you.

 

The queen showed the box to the mirror. 'Ha! That can't be!

Snow White is dead. Here is her heart!' But the mirror answered:

 

Oh, Queen, the fairest in this room are you, but Snow White beyond the Seven Mountains in the house of the

Seven Dwarfs is a thousand times fairer than you!

 

The queen understood that the huntsman had fooled her. She trembled with rage.


“Then I'll kill her myself,” she said, gritting her teeth.

 

She went down six flights of stairs to the cellar and there conjured up a poisoned apple. It was red on one side and green on the other. She changed herself into an old woman and set off, over the seven mountains, to the house of the seven dwarves.

"I'll get her!" she growled again and again. And in her basket, among the other apples, lay the poisoned apple... Snow White, who was left alone in

the cottage of the seven dwarfs, was just about to put a pan of soup on the fire, when there was a tap at the window. It was an old woman, who was carrying a basket of apples.

 

'May I rest here for a while? I am so tired,' she panted. Snow White hesitated. She opened the window and said, 'I am sorry, old woman, the dwarves will not let anyone in. But I can give you a glass of water.' When the witch had

finished the glass, she said, 'Since you have been so kind to me, I will give you an apple. Look, a very beautiful red-green apple. Take a nice

bite!' Snow White took a bite and… fell dead on the ground.

 

 

 

'Hahahaha,' laughed the wicked queen (for that is what it was of course!), 'hahahaha! Now I am the fairest of them all again!' And she hurried back to her

palace, on the other side of the seven mountains.

When the dwarfs came home they found Snow White lying dead on the floor. The dwarfs were deeply and deeply sad, so sad that they could not say goodbye to her. They made a beautiful glass coffin for her and put her in it, so that they could always look at her.

 

A few days later a prince happened to be riding through the forest on his horse and saw the glass coffin. He thought Snow White was so beautiful that he lifted the glass lid and gave her a kiss.

 

Apparently Snow White was not completely dead, because she started coughing in fright and the poisoned piece of apple shot out of her throat.

She sat up in surprise and asked, "Where am I? What happened?"


“Hurray!” shouted the dwarves.

How happy they were that Snow White was alive again! The prince was too. He took Snow White on his horse and they rode off together to his palace.

There they got married and lived happily ever after.

 

And the evil queen? She heard from the mirror that Snow White was still the most beautiful. She found that so terrible that she dropped dead.

Yes, really, she fell over in sheer rage, just like that, right there in front of the mirror.

A long, long time ago, there lived a boy so small and thin that everyone called him Little Thumb. He had six older brothers, who were also just as skinny as Little Thumb, because their parents were so poor that they couldn’t afford enough food for them. The father was a woodcutter, but no one bought his wood since everyone in the area was just as poor.

One night, Little Thumb’s stomach growled so loudly that he couldn’t sleep. He snuck downstairs to see if he could find a few crumbs of bread. In the kitchen, he overheard his father sighing, “Wife, we’re completely out of food. There’s no other choice. Tomorrow, I’ll take the children to the forest and leave them there.” Little Thumb couldn’t believe his ears—who would do something so terrible? But that’s how things go in fairy tales.

Little though he was, he was also very clever, and he quickly came up with a plan. He snuck outside and filled his pockets with pebbles. The next day, his father said, “We’re all going to the forest.” So, off they went, one after the other in a long line, with the father leading and Little Thumb at the very end. Every five steps, he secretly dropped a pebble. When they reached a stream, the father stopped. “I’m going to cut wood on the other side. You stay here and play, and I’ll come back to get you later.”When the sun had set and the father still hadn’t returned, the brothers began to cry. Little Thumb said, “Don’t worry, I know the way home.” And he did, for he simply followed the pebbles he had dropped along the way.The father and mother were very happy to see them because that day the father had managed to sell some wood, and there was bread in the house once again.

But after a while, things went wrong again. No one bought wood from the woodcutter, and once again, they all sat around the table with empty plates. So, the next day, the father took the children to the forest once more, and again, Little Thumb had filled his pockets with pebbles, which he dropped along the way. Once again, he found his way home in the evening. By chance, the father had earned a little money that day, and everyone was happy again.

But even this happiness didn’t last long. One day, there was only half a loaf of bread in the house, which was far too little for nine people. Little Thumb knew what he had to do, but when he tried to gather pebbles from the yard that night, he discovered to his dismay that the door was locked! “We can’t stay here all night,” said Little Thumb. “Let’s try to find our own way back.” But they got lost and wandered deeper into the forest, farther and farther from home. They reached a large, tall house, much bigger than their own. Little Thumb knocked on the door. You know what? There’s an extra bed in the room next to where his seven daughters sleep. You can spend the night there. Quickly, upstairs!” Tripping over each other, Little Thumb and his brothers hurried up the steep stairs, following the woman. She led them into a room. “Get in bed, and remember: not a peep! His daughters are sleeping next door, and the giant mustn’t hear you.” She turned and went downstairs to let the giant in. Little Thumb and his brothers climbed into the large bed and held their breath. They heard the giant come in—or rather, they felt it, as the whole house shook. “What do I smell?” they heard him say. “Do I smell human flesh?” “No, no,” the woman replied nervously, “you’re just imagining things.”

Little Thumb quietly got out of bed and snuck over to the room where the giant’s daughters were sleeping. They lay in one big bed, each wearing a small crown. Gently, Little Thumb took their crowns and crept back to his brothers. “Put these crowns on!” he whispered, and before hiding under the blanket, he put one on his own head.

A bit later, the giant stomped up the stairs. “And yet I smell human flesh,” he muttered as he entered the room where Little Thumb and his brothers lay. In the dark, he felt the crowns and assumed they were his daughters. Still not completely sure, he went into the next room. “See? I did smell human flesh,” he said, feeling a head of hair and then, one by one, ate all his seven daughters, clothes and all, so quickly that they didn’t even wake up! A long, long time ago, there was an emperor who was incredibly vain. Now, everyone is a little vain, but this emperor loved looking in the mirror way too much—and, on top of that, he was quite foolish. Just listen.

The emperor in this story did nothing but try on new clothes all day. In fact, he thought about nothing but clothes. He had twelve huge closets, all completely full, and mirrors were everywhere in his palace. Each hallway had at least three, the dining room had eight, and the grand salon had ten—let alone the bedroom. There was a giant mirror fastened to the ceiling so that he could look at himself even while lying in bed. As you can imagine, he spent a lot of time lying on his back.

Everyone in the land talked about how vain the emperor was. Two strangers who happened to be traveling through the country and were out of money heard about this. They came up with a clever plan to make a lot of money easily.

They went to the palace and told the emperor they could make clothes that were not only beautiful but also very special. The emperor sat up straight immediately—he had to have those clothes! “How so, special?” he asked. “Only smart people can see the clothes,” explained the men. “For foolish people, they’re invisible.”

These clothes are not only beautiful and special, thought the emperor, but also very useful because now I’ll know who is foolish and who isn’t. And you can see that the emperor himself wasn’t very bright because who would believe such a thing? The emperor ordered the two liars to start immediately, as in two weeks, there would be a grand parade through the city, and he wanted to lead it in his new clothes. He showed them to a large room where they could set up their looms. The two men asked the emperor for heaps of money to buy the finest, most luxurious, and therefore the most expensive threads. But they kept the money for themselves. They didn’t buy anything. Day after day, they sat at the looms, pretending to string threads, pretending to weave, pretending to pull the finest fabrics off the looms, and pretending to make the most beautiful clothes from these fabrics. But there were no fabrics, there were no clothes—there was nothing at all.

Of course, the emperor was very curious about how the clothes looked. He sent his chief minister to check. One of the men showed him in and pointed to the empty looms and the bare tables. “Just look,” he said, “do you see how beautifully the fabric shines? And do you see how splendid the clothes on the table turned out?”

The chief minister looked at the empty looms and the empty tables and thought, Oh dear, I must be foolish because I don’t see any clothes. But he didn’t say this out loud because he didn’t want everyone to know he was foolish. So he said, “Yes, yes, yes! They’re truly magnificent!”

And when the emperor later asked how the clothes were, the chief minister again said they were magnificent. “Aha!” the emperor thought to himself. “This minister isn’t foolish, for he can see the clothes.”

Everyone in the land heard about the two marvelous tailors, and everyone talked about the emperor’s new clothes. The emperor grew more and more curious, but he had to wait until the day of the big parade. Only then would he finally be allowed to admire his new clothes.

With all his ministers in a line behind him, he went to the room of the two fraudsters. Of course, the emperor couldn’t see the clothes. Oh no, he thought, am I foolish too? And naturally, he didn’t want anyone to know that either. “Well, well, well,” he said, “they are truly stunning!” “Yes, yes, yes,” said all the ministers, who also saw nothing. “They are truly stunning!”

The emperor then undressed completely to put on his new clothes. The two men pretended to dress him. First, they pretended to put on underwear, then a shirt, pants, a jacket, and the rest of the ensemble. Then they took a step back and looked at the naked emperor from head to toe. “Yes,” they said, “the clothes truly suit you perfectly!” “Yes, yes, yes,” all the ministers chimed in, “the clothes truly suit you perfectly!”

Proudly, the emperor walked outside and, stark naked, paraded confidently at the head of the procession. The streets were packed with people, all eager to see the emperor’s new clothes, and everyone shouted, “Oh, oh, oh, what magnificent clothes!” Because no one wanted to appear foolish, of course.

But suddenly, a little boy called out, “But he isn’t wearing any clothes at all!”

Startled, the people around the boy looked at him. Then they looked at each other again. And you know what happens when one person dares to tell the truth? Suddenly, others dare to as well. So a moment later, everyone was shouting, “The emperor is walking around naked! The emperor is walking around naked!”

Clearly, I’m not the only foolish one, thought the emperor. He straightened his back and marched even more proudly through the crowd. Completely naked! Can you imagine that?

A long, long time ago, there was a mother goat who lived with her seven little kids in a cottage at the edge of the forest. Very often, three times a week even, Mother Goat had to go to the village to buy groceries, because seven little goats sure eat a lot! And every time before leaving, she would say, “Listen well, children, be careful of the wolf! I don’t need to tell you what wolves do to little goats. So lock the door tightly when I’m gone. You must only open it for me and no one else. Do you understand?” She was so careful! And she was right, just listen...

One day, Mother Goat had to go shopping again. This time, someone was hiding behind the big tree next to the cottage, watching her until she disappeared around the bend. That “someone” crept up to the cottage, sniffed around the walls and the windows. You guessed it—it was the wolf! He knocked on the door and said in his sweetest voice, “Open up, my dear little goats, it’s your mother.” Inside, the kids huddled together in fear and replied, “No, no, you don’t sound like our mother. Our mother doesn’t have such a gruff voice!” They squeezed closer together and shivered with fright. Oh, that’s right, thought the wolf, I need to speak in a higher voice. He walked back to the big tree, waited a moment, then returned to the door.With a high-pitched voice, he said, “Dear little goats, open up, it’s your mother.” It did sound like their mother, but just to be sure, the kids called out, “Then show us your paw under the door!” They gasped and shrank back when they saw a brown, furry paw through the crack under the door! “No, no,” they cried, trembling with wide eyes, “you’re not our mother! Our mother has white paws!” Oh, that’s right, thought the wolf. He went to his den and came back with a pot of flour. He dipped a paw in it and then said in a high voice, “Dear little goats, open the door! It’s your mother.” The little goats all gathered by the door and called, “Then show us your paw!”


When they saw the white paw, they opened the door. And they should not have done that… The wolf sprang inside. The little goats screamed and scattered in every direction. One hid under the table, the second behind the cupboard, the third under the bed, the fourth in the laundry basket, the fifth behind the coat rack, the sixth behind a door, and the seventh in the grandfather clock… The wolf found them all and gobbled them up right away—all but one... The only one he missed was the seventh little kid hiding in the clock.

Not long after, Mother Goat came back. From a distance, she saw that the door was wide open, and when she found the flour pot near the door and couldn’t see her kids anywhere, she began to wail in despair. “My little ones, my little ones,” she cried, “where are you?”

The seventh little goat crept out of the clock and told her what had happened. “One thing is certain,” said Mother Goat, “if he ate six of my kids, he must have a very full belly now, and with such a big belly, he won’t get far.” She took a pair of scissors and a sewing kit and went outside. Flour was scattered everywhere, so she could easily see where the wolf had gone. And sure enough, there he was, sleeping under a tree not too far away.

“Look at that big belly of his,” Mother Goat said to herself, “that’s where my darlings are.” She tiptoed closer and, very quickly, snipped open his belly. The little goats sprang out. Mother Goat filled the wolf’s belly with big stones and sewed it up again nice and tight. Then, they all hid behind the trees.

After a long while, the wolf woke up. I feel so strange in my belly, he thought, and I’m so thirsty! He walked to a brook, which was very hard to do with all those stones in his belly, leaned over to drink, fell into the water, and drowned. Mother Goat and her seven little kids jumped up and down with joy—they were so happy that the wicked wolf was gone. Relieved, they all went home. They took the flour pot with them and made delicious pancakes from it.

It was quite far, but Little Red Riding Hood knew the way very well. “Remember,” said her mother, “walk briskly and don’t stray from the path.” Little Red Riding Hood promised and set off through the forest, walking at a steady pace.

Suddenly, a big wolf poked his head out from between the trees. Little Red Riding Hood greeted him politely but kept walking briskly. “Young lady, young lady,” said the wolf, “why the rush? Where are you going with that basket?”“To Grandma’s,” replied Little Red Riding Hood. “She’s sick.”

“Oh? And where does Grandma live?” asked the wolf again.“Near the big oak tree,” said Little Red Riding Hood, “but if you don’t mind, I have to go now. Goodbye, Mr. Wolf!”

The wolf walked alongside her and said, “Oh, come on, don’t you see what a beautiful day it is today? And look at all those lovely flowers. I’m sure Grandma would be very happy if you brought her a bunch of flowers along with that basket of treats.”

The wolf was right; those flowers were beautiful. She forgot her mother’s advice and… wandered off the path.

Meanwhile, the wolf dashed through the forest toward the big oak tree. He arrived at Grandma’s cottage and stopped outside. “Grandma,” he called, “it’s Little Red Riding Hood. “But Grandma, what a big mouth you have!” Little Red Riding Hood was amazed. “All the better to eat you with!” cried the wolf, who by now was ravenously hungry. He sprang up and gobbled Little Red Riding Hood in one big bite, just as he had done with Grandma. Satisfied, he lay back down in bed, immediately fell asleep (that’s what happens when you eat so much!), and started snoring very loudly.

Without another thought, he pulled out his hunting knife and cut open the wolf’s belly. Out stepped Grandma and Little Red Riding Hood. “Oh, whew, it was so stuffy in there,” they sighed in relief. “Shhh!” whispered the hunter. “We don’t want to wake the wolf.”

The hunter and Little Red Riding Hood each grabbed a chair, and the three of them enjoyed the bread rolls, cookies, and wine that Little Red Riding Hood’s mother had packed.

ONCE upon a time, there was a king who had eleven daughters. Was he happy with them? Yes and no. Yes, because he was rather proud of all those beautiful girls. And no, because they gave him a headache. He was faced with a mystery. For weeks on end, he had to buy new shoes for his eleven daughters every single day. Eleven pairs of shoes each day, that’s seventy-seven pairs of shoes in one week. And why? Because the shoes he bought the previous day were already worn out the next: the soles were falling off, the heels were worn down unevenly, the straps were torn, and so on.
How could this be? How could those shoes be completely worn out and destroyed in just one night? And it was even stranger because the king locked his daughters in their bedroom every evening precisely at eight o'clock. They couldn’t go anywhere!

When he asked them about it, the eleven daughters would lower their eyes and stay silent in every language. The king was completely driven mad by this. And all those shoes also cost him a fortune. At his wit's end, the king placed an advertisement in the newspaper.

Whoever could solve the mystery of the worn-out shoes would be allowed to marry one of the princesses. You could spend three nights in the girls' bedroom, but if you hadn’t solved the mystery by then, your head would be chopped off.

Weeks went by. Many men had tried to solve the mystery, but each one of them fell asleep at some point and could not explain how those shoes became worn out. One soldier, who was no longer young and had been injured in the war, wanted to give it a try. But first, he went to a wise old woman for advice. The woman gave him a cloak that would make him invisible when he put it on. And she also gave him some wise advice: “Above all, be careful not to drink anything!” The soldier didn’t entirely understand, but he decided to follow her advice anyway.

The next day, he knocked on the palace gate. The king let him in and said he hoped that the mystery would finally be solved. When the clock struck eight, the princesses went to bed. The soldier received the key to the bedroom and locked himself in with the princesses. He put the key in his pocket.

Meanwhile, the girls were getting ready for the night. The youngest offered
the soldier a glass of red wine. “You were injured, weren't you?” she said kindly. “You must
need some strengthening. A good glass of wine will do you good!”

The soldier was very thirsty and was just about to take a sip when he remembered
what the wise woman had said: “Above all, be careful not to drink anything!” So he secretly poured the
glass into a vase. Moments later, the youngest princess showed him to a nice soft bed. The soldier lay down and
closed his eyes. The magic cloak was rolled up beside him.

The light went out, and for a long time, nothing happened. The soldier decided to pretend to sleep and began to snore. And sure enough, the light came on again. He heard whispering and rustling all around. “That old man would have fallen asleep even without our sleeping potion,” joked one of the princesses. The soldier carefully opened one eye and saw that the girls were all taking ball gowns out of their wardrobes. He thought she was the most beautiful.She wasn’t exactly thrilled, because she thought he was far too old. But alas, if you are a princess, you can’t just marry whomever you want. No, your father chooses for you.

LONG, LONG AGO, there was once a miller who was very proud of his daughter. He thought she was the most beautiful, sweetest, and cleverest girl in the whole world. He boasted that she could do anything – whatever you could think of, she could do it.

“Of course, she can,” said the miller without blinking an eye.

He shouldn’t have said that, because a) it wasn’t true, and b) that person told the king. And this king was very greedy, especially when it came to gold. So, the girl had to come to the palace immediately.

The king took her to a little room in the attic. Three bundles of straw lay on the floor, and there was a spinning wheel. “I’ll be back tomorrow,” he said, “and if you haven’t spun this straw into gold, I’ll have your head chopped off.” He left the room and locked the door behind him.

As tears streamed down her face, she suddenly heard a tiny voice say, “Why are you crying, girl?” In front of her stood a very small, dwarfish man.

“I can help you,” said the little man, “for I can spin gold from straw. But as a reward, I want that necklace around your neck.”

“That’s fine,” said the girl. She quickly took off her necklace and gave it to him.

The little man put her to sleep, and when she awoke the next morning, she had to squint her eyes shut because the room was gleaming with gold!

---

He had six more bundles of straw brought in and said, “I’ll be back tomorrow, and if this straw hasn’t turned to gold, I’ll have your head chopped off.” And once again, he locked the door.

The girl started crying again, and there was the dwarf again. This time, he wanted her bracelet. After he got it, he put her to sleep again and transformed the straw into gold.

But even with six piles of gold, the king wasn’t satisfied. Now she was taken to a larger room, stuffed to the ceiling with straw.

“Until tomorrow!” he said to the girl. “You know what happens if you don’t spin it into gold. But if you succeed, you may marry the prince tomorrow.” And after those words, he locked the door. The girl was desperate. What if the dwarf didn’t come? But there he was again.

“I have nothing left to give you,” the girl cried. “You already took my necklace and bracelet.”

-----

The little man thought for a moment. “If I understand correctly,” he said, “you get to marry the prince if I turn the straw into gold. Fine with me, but as a reward, I want your first child.”

The girl was so frightened about losing her head that she didn’t think twice and quickly said, “Yes, yes, I promise!”

The dwarf put her to sleep again, and the next day, all the straw was turned to gold.

“I’ve come for my reward,” he said.

The girl began to cry terribly because she loved her child deeply, but that made no impression on the little man. “A promise is a promise,” he said.

The girl fell to her knees and offered him all the necklaces and bracelets she had if she could only keep the baby. The little man finally took pity on her and said, “Alright, I’ll give you a chance. The princess immediately asked all the footmen, maidservants, and ladies-in-waiting in the palace for their names and wrote them all down. The next day, when the dwarf came to the palace, the princess was ready with a long list of names. “Well?” said the dwarf. “What’s my name?”

The princess started at the top of the list. “Is your name Frederick?”

“No,” said the little man.

“No,” he replied.

The princess now had twenty times as many names as the first time.

Each time, the little man replied, “No!”

“Well?” he said. “This is your last chance. What’s my name?”

The princess pretended to read from a long list of names and began: “Are you… Dabberbeard?”

“No, no,” said the little man, rubbing his hands, “no, that’s not it.”

That was terrible for the little man, but the princess was thrilled. She took her baby from the crib and danced around the room.

The girl took out a stack of paper from the drawer. “I’ve already collected many names. We can choose from these.”

The prince kissed her and said, “That’s what I find so special about you, you always think of everything.”

And so, the miller’s daughter lived happily ever after with her prince and her baby.

LONG, LONG AGO, there was a woman who had two daughters. One was blonde, the other had black hair, and for reasons unknown to me, she loved the blonde daughter very much and disliked the other. This was despite the fact that the blonde girl was terribly lazy and grumpy, while the dark-haired girl was sweet and kind!

---

One day, the dark-haired girl was polishing the cutlery again. To her dismay, a little spoon slipped from her hands and fell into the well. The mother became furious. “You’d better get that thing back!” she snapped. “I don’t care how!” And she went back to her embroidery.

---

The poor girl, who was terrified of her mother, tightly shut her eyes and jumped into the deep well. She fell and fell, and she kept falling, and during that fall, she, believe it or not, fell asleep!

After a while, the man said, “You’re going to the market to sell your cow because you have no food left for your wife and children.”

In response, the strange man pulled a bottle out of his pocket. “Look,” he said, “if you give me that cow, I will give you this bottle.”

“That’s true,” said the man, “but this is no ordinary bottle. It’s a magic bottle.”

You see, thought the farmer, a magic bottle! So it is indeed a wizard!

“Yes, I think so too,” said the strange man. “But if she starts nagging, just tell her to sweep the floor, set a tablecloth on the table, and place the bottle in the middle of the table. Then you’ll see something. Just believe me. You’re a good man, that’s why I’m doing this for you.”

A LONG, LONG time ago, in a place far away, there was a miller who had three sons. “When I die,” he always said, “my oldest son shall have the mill, the second my donkey, and the third my cat.”

So when the man was dead and buried, the oldest son moved into the mill, the second son received the donkey, and the youngest son got the cat.

“Well, this is something,” said the youngest son to himself. “My oldest brother can earn money with the mill, my second brother can earn money with the donkey, but how am I supposed to earn money with a cat?” He sighed deeply and looked sadly ahead. The cat walked over to him on its soft paws and, to the boy's great astonishment, began to speak! “Maybe it’s true,” said the cat, “that you can’t earn money with me, but believe it or not, I can make you rich.” Of course, the boy didn’t believe him.

“First, buy me a nice pair of boots,” said the cat.

---

What does a cat with four legs need with a pair of boots? thought the boy. But he can talk, so who knows what else he can do? With his last coins, he bought a pair of boots.

The cat stood up on his hind legs, put on the boots, and looked at himself in the mirror. He took a few steps to the left and a few steps to the right, nodded approvingly, put his owner’s hat on his head, and walked out the door to carry out his plan. Because he had a plan! A plan to make his master the richest man in the land.

---

He headed towards the river, where he caught two fat carp. With them, he went to the king’s palace. To one of the attendants, he said, “Tell the king that these carp are a gift from the Marquis of Carabas.”

---

The next day, the cat caught two partridges. Again he went to the king’s palace. “Give these to the king,” said the cat to the attendant, “and say that it’s a gift from the Marquis of Carabas.”

“The king says he wants to speak to you himself,” said the attendant, and he brought the cat to the king.

The king greeted the cat warmly: “Well, well, another gift from the Marquis of Carabas. He must be a very nice man. Would you please send him my regards and thank him warmly?”

Not long after, the cat heard that the king, along with his daughter, the princess, would take a carriage ride along the lake.

---

The cat hurried to his master. “Get dressed quickly; we’re going to the lake.”

---

Once there, they hid behind some bushes. “If you want to become rich, do exactly as I say,” whispered the cat. The miller’s son didn’t understand anything, but he nodded obediently. After all, he wanted to be rich!

---

After a while, they heard the sound of hoofbeats: the king’s carriage was approaching. “Quick, jump into the water, now!” whispered the cat, and he ran out onto the road, waving his paws wildly. “Help, help!” he shouted. “The Marquis of Carabas is drowning, the Marquis of Carabas is drowning! Help, help!”

The king ordered the carriage to stop and said, “The Marquis of Carabas? Isn’t that the nice gentleman who gave me those carp and partridges? We must help him immediately.”

The miller’s son was pulled from the water and given dry clothes. But that was not all. He was allowed to ride in the coach and sat next to the princess.

---

The cat did not wait for any of this and had already run ahead. When he reached the sprawling wheat fields, he asked the farmers working there, “Whose wheat fields are these?”

---

“They belong to the great wizard who lives in the castle beyond the forest,” one of the farmers replied, pointing into the distance.

The cat said, “The king will come by soon. If he asks whose wheat fields these are, you must say, ‘They belong to the Marquis of Carabas.’ If you don’t, I’ll bite your head off.”

The farmers were a little afraid of a talking cat that walked upright like a person and wore boots, so they promised to do as he said. And off went the cat again.

---

Soon after, the king’s carriage arrived at the wheat fields, and indeed, the king asked the farmers whose beautiful fields they were.

“They belong to the Marquis of Carabas,” they said.

“Well, well,” said the king, looking at the miller’s son in surprise. “Well, well,” he said again, and the princess smiled kindly at him.

The miller’s son was, of course, as surprised as the king, but he said nothing.

---

Meanwhile, the cat had arrived at the great forest and asked the ranger working there, “Whose forest is this?”

“It belongs to the great wizard who lives in the castle, beyond that hill,” replied the ranger.

The cat said, “The king will come by soon. If he asks whose forest this is, you must say, ‘It belongs to the Marquis of Carabas.’ And if you don’t, I’ll bite your head off.”

The ranger was a bit afraid of a cat in boots, so he promised to say that. And the cat ran on.

---

Then the king’s carriage arrived at the forest. The king leaned out of the window and asked the ranger whose forest it was.

“It belongs to the Marquis of Carabas,” said the ranger.

“Well, well, well,” said the king, nodding kindly at the miller’s son. “Well, well, well.”

The princess smiled and moved a little closer to the boy.

---

Meanwhile, the cat continued running, and now he arrived at the wizard’s castle. It was an enormous castle, twice as large and beautiful as the king’s. Now, this wizard was a very nasty man. No one dared to go near his castle, for everyone could tell you that he had once turned a hundred people into worms—just like that, because he was in a bad mood. But the booted cat was not afraid at all; he simply walked right in. Clack, clack went the boots on the shiny marble floor, and the wizard, who was sitting behind a large table, looked up in surprise.

The cat made a deep bow. “Oh, great wizard,” he said, “everyone says you are a very powerful sorcerer, but you know, people say so much.”

“So, you don’t believe that?” said the wizard. “Well, just watch!” No sooner had he said that than he vanished, and in the wizard’s place stood a great roaring lion, which tried to crush the cat with its claws. But the cat had expected something like this and quickly ran away, into the curtains.

“Wonderful, wonderful!” cried the cat from above. “You truly can work magic. But can you also turn yourself into something small? A mouse, for example?”

At that very moment, the lion disappeared, and a little mouse scurried across the floor squeaking.

“Now I’ve got you,” said the cat, and in one leap, he was down and gobbled up the mouse, fur and all.

---

The royal coach had now arrived at the castle. The king leaned out of the window to see whom he could ask whose beautiful castle this was. To his surprise, the booted cat came outside.

“Your royal highness,” said the cat with a deep bow, “welcome to the castle of the Marquis of Carabas.”

“Well, well, well,” said the king, looking at the miller’s son with admiration.

“Well, well, well,” said the princess, extending her hand to the miller’s son, who helped her out of the coach. Together they walked into the castle. Everyone was amazed—especially the miller’s son! What splendor and riches: marble and gold everywhere!

---

“Your castle is even more beautiful and larger than mine,” said the king to the miller’s son. “I happen to be looking for a man for my daughter,” he continued, “and I think you would be very suitable for that.”

---

The princess nodded three, four times in succession, for she thought so too.

---

Soon a wedding was celebrated, and the bride and groom moved into the magnificent castle.

And the cat? He got his own room and twenty attendants who did nothing all day but take care of him. He also received a wardrobe full of boots, in all the colors of the rainbow.

---

Everything was now rosy and bright, except that the princess could never understand why her husband spoiled that cat so much. Twenty attendants, she thought that was really excessive!

But, well, he couldn’t explain it to her.
